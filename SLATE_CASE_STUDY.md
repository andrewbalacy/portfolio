# SLATE Case Study

## Overview

Slate began with a problem I was experiencing every day.

Working 10-hour shifts as a Medical Assistant, I still wanted to improve my fitness, learn software engineering, spend time with family and friends, and continue building toward long-term goals.

The motivation was never the issue.

The decision was.

Every evening I found myself staring at a long list of things I wanted to accomplish, but after mentally demanding days, I had no clear starting point. Some days I had the energy to do everything. Other days I had almost none.

Traditional productivity systems expected both days to look the same.

Real life doesn't.

---

# The Problem

> "I wasn't struggling to stay productive. I was struggling to decide where to begin."

Traditional productivity apps assume your capacity is constant.

Life proves otherwise.

Stress, recovery, sleep, work, unexpected responsibilities, and mental fatigue all influence what we're realistically capable of accomplishing on any given day.

When every task feels equally important, deciding where to begin becomes mentally exhausting.

For me, that usually led to one of two outcomes:

- Trying to do everything and burning out.
- Doing nothing and feeling guilty.

Neither moved me closer to the life I wanted.

---

# The Insight

The breakthrough wasn't another productivity system.

It was realizing that execution should adapt to capacity.

Instead of asking:

> "What do I want to accomplish today?"

I began asking:

> "Given my current capacity, what can I realistically execute today?"

That single shift changed everything.

Rather than forcing perfect plans onto imperfect days, the plan itself became adaptive.

---

# The First Prototype

Ironically, Slate didn't begin as software.

It began inside ChatGPT Projects.

Every evening I manually entered my recovery, mental energy, available time, priorities, and daily constraints into a carefully designed prompt.

The recommendations were genuinely useful.

The workflow wasn't.

I found myself copying and pasting the same prompt every single day.

Eventually I realized something.

If I was repeating the same workflow every evening...

I should build the tool instead.

---

# The Solution

Slate is a capacity-aware execution intelligence platform built around one simple belief:

**Better decisions lead to better execution.**

Rather than overwhelming users with more tasks, Slate helps identify the most realistic path forward based on their current state.

Its purpose isn't to maximize productivity.

It's to maximize sustainable execution.

The objective isn't doing everything.

It's consistently doing the right things.

---

# Building the Product

As Slate evolved, it became much more than a daily planner.

It grew into an intelligent decision-support platform featuring:

- Capacity-aware planning
- Recovery and health insights
- Recommendation confidence scoring
- Explainable decision making
- Tradeoff analysis
- Tomorrow capacity projections
- Pattern detection
- Adaptive execution recommendations

Every feature exists because it solved a real problem I encountered while using previous versions.

Nothing was added simply because it could be.

Every addition earned its place.

---

# Engineering Philosophy

Building Slate taught me something unexpected.

I enjoy thinking in systems.

Rather than focusing on isolated features, I became fascinated by designing how those features interact to create a cohesive experience.

That philosophy influenced every engineering decision.

- Feature-based architecture
- Shared business logic
- Explainable recommendations
- Continuous iteration
- Thorough documentation
- Long-term maintainability over short-term speed

Building Slate became just as much an exercise in software engineering as it was in product design.

---

# Looking Forward

Today, Slate is still evolving.

The long-term vision extends far beyond personal productivity.

I believe the future of intelligent software isn't simply helping people do more.

It's helping people make better decisions.

One day I'd love to see platforms like WHOOP, Bevel, and other health technology companies integrate capacity-aware execution into their ecosystems.

Not as a replacement.

As a complement.

Understanding how recovered you are is valuable.

Understanding what to do with that recovery is equally important.
